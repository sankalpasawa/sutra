# Feedback: Onboarding Intake Feels Heavy

**Date**: 2026-04-03
**Source**: Founder during new company onboarding (Phase 1: INTAKE)
**Severity**: UX friction — affects first impression

## What Happened

Founder's reaction to the 11-question intake: "It takes time to start."

The onboarding dumps all 11 questions at once. Before the founder has written a single line of code or seen any value from Sutra, they're asked to articulate their bet, name a persona, define a 2-year vision. That's a lot of cognitive load upfront.

## What Principle Is Missing

Sutra's onboarding violates a basic product principle: **show value before asking for investment.** The founder came with energy and an idea. The first thing Sutra does is hand them a homework assignment.

## Founder's Direct Feedback (second round)

> "These are too many questions at this stage. Make it feel engaging so that I haven't thought things out. Make it two and fro till you get all the answers."

The founder explicitly wants:
- **No question dump.** Not even 3 at once.
- **Back-and-forth.** One question, one answer, react, next question.
- **Designed for founders who haven't fully thought it out.** Sutra should HELP them think, not quiz them.
- **Engaging.** It should feel like talking to a sharp co-founder, not filling out a form.

## Required Change to CLIENT-ONBOARDING.md

Phase 1 (INTAKE) must be rewritten as a conversational flow:

1. Start with ONE question: "What do you want to build?"
2. React to the answer — reflect back what you heard, add insight.
3. Ask the NEXT natural question based on what they said.
4. Build the Intake Card progressively as answers come in.
5. Never show all 11 questions at once.
6. Infer what you can (platform, skills) from context. Only ask when you can't infer.

The 10 questions still exist as a checklist for Sutra internally — but the founder never sees them as a list.

## What We Learned

Sutra's intake must be a conversation, not a questionnaire. The founder may not have answers ready — Sutra's job is to help them find the answers through dialogue, not demand them upfront.
